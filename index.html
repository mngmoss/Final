FOREWORD The dramatic images of the destructive attack on Iraq and the fall of the hated regime of Saddam Hussein are etched into our mind's eye. Iraq dominated the news for months, led to unbridge- able political contradictions, and elicited vehement reactions all over the world.

East and West, millions of people demonstrated against war. Yet worldwide, millions of people were also concerned about the oppression in Iraq and about the threat of new weapons and new terror attacks. In the meantime, Iraqis feared to fall victim to violence once again: to the violence of war, chaos and oppression.

More than one year later, the people of Iraq still know no peace. The rejoicing about the fall of Saddam Hussein was short-lived. The damage to international relations still has not been repaired. Uncertainty and fear still hold civilians and political leaders in their grip the world over.

The Iraqi people has faced many ordeals. She was subjected to a cruel dictatorship and suffered from crippling economic sanctions. She survived many wars imposed upon her. An imposed liberation changed into a hated occupation in the eyes of many. Iraqis still have no voice. And it is Iraqis who are still paying the highest price: with the loss of loved ones, with the destruction of their country, and with insults to their national dignity. The result has been fear and anger, despair and bitter disappointment.

Politicians and concerned citizens who stood against war and oppression stand a lot to learn from the questions and answers presented by the Iraqi people. After years of being gagged, Iraqis must be returned their voice and future. Politicians and concerned citizens the world over should now heed their calls for peace and justice.

The photographs of Geert van Kesteren bring alive the experience of common Iraqis, confronted with uncommon violence. His photo- graphs prompt us to look through the eyes of the people of Iraq, and listen to the voice of the Iraqi people. It is only then that those respon- sible to protect the innocent against war and oppression can contribute to an Iraq as envisioned by Iraqis.

Jan Gruiters

Director, Pax Christi Netherlands

Iraqis Uncover
Thousands in
Mass Graves

The text discusses the tragic events surrounding the 1992 uprising against Saddam Hussein's regime in southern Iraq, focusing on the account of Iskandar Jawad Witwit, the Mayor of Hilla. After the Gulf War, prompted by calls from then-American President George H.W. Bush, both Kurds in the north and Shiites in the south rose against Saddam's oppressive rule. However, the U.S. failed to support the rebellion, allowing Saddam to regain control, resulting in mass killings of civilians. In Hilla and neighboring Al Mahawil, thousands were executed and buried in mass graves, while U.S. military helicopters watched without intervening.

The brutal suppression of the uprising marked a devastating chapter in Iraq's history, with an estimated 250,000 lives lost during Saddam Hussein's regime. This account underscores the broken promise of international support and the tragic consequences of the failed uprising, shedding light on the suffering of innocent civilians, including women, children, and the disabled. It also highlights the geopolitical complexities and the profound impact of international decisions on the lives of those caught in the midst of conflict and turmoil.

Dead Men Tell
Tales in Iraq

The Iraqis have been searching for missing family members among the bones, skulls and clothing of the excavated skeletons in Al Mahawil for four days already. An excavator digs a hole in the ground and then volunteers haul out the bones with their bare hands. Everyone is doing their best, but in fact they are just doing something. I see a young man who is helping them walk past with a skull in his hands. He doesn't know to which skeleton it belongs.

In the end he just puts down the skull with any old stack of bones. The remains are then crammed into a plastic bag. Sometimes people find an identity card in the pockets of the cloth- ing, but often there is nothing. There are people who believe they can recognize their missing son from the brand of cigarettes that he smoked.

In tears, the pockets are searched through time and again by desperate family members. Time and time again they search through the bag with bones in the hope of recognizing anything from their missed ones. Usually they find nothing, but sometimes you hear loud screaming and wailing somewhere, or you see someone fall faint: a son, daughter or grandchild has been found.

Iraq Shias
Massacred on
Holy Day

Outside, near the wall of the shrine in Karbala, you can hear men and women wailing in despair. Even after more than 1300 years, the Shiites still mourn the brutal killing of Imam al-Hussein, grandson of the prophet Mohammed. During processions, men beat their breasts with their hands while they chant his name. On the final day of Ashoura they make an incision on their heads with a sword and then beat the wound so that the blood flows freely. This is how they express their enduring readiness to sacrifice their own blood for Hussein.

It is around midday when I hear five dull thuds. I run towards where they came from. A policeman suddenly grabs me from behind. He is in a fury. In the distance I see something that looks like a bomb attack. A militiaman runs at me yelling and whacks me in the face, which I can half duck. Another man pulls at my camera. I resist and get a thump in the stomach. Then the man loads his Kalashnikov. A crowd of people start getting involved, which makes the situation even more complicated. 'Calm, calm, friend,' I call out in Arabic. I just got here. No photo, no photo. Peace.' The armed man smashes the barrel of his gun straight into my lens.

Then, out of nowhere, a massive man walks up. He seizes me in an armlock and drags me away from the excited masses. Twenty metres on he whispers in my ear: 'I am your friend.' He says that he comes from Iran. Once we are a long way off he lets me free, gives me a big wink and says: 'Bye bye.' He quickly takes a left, and I go right. There are also bomb explosions in Baghdad. The total number of fatalities exceeds 200. The attention in the Western media was massively overshadowed by that for the bomb attacks in Madrid nine days later, in which a similar number of people lost their lives.

The blackest day for the UN, Kofi Annan called it. It was the day when everything changed in Iraq. The day when civilians became the target of terrorists. The day when journalists and employees of NGOs (non- governmental organisations) lost their independence, their immunity. It is the day of the attack on the UN headquarters, the Canal Hotel in Baghdad. Sergio Vieira de Mello was in charge of the UN mission. An independent UN mission to help Iraq become a country unified, a mission to keep a watchful eye on the behaviour of the Americans, a mission to help with reconstruction.

Until August 19, 2003, when the driver of a cement-mixer truck with 1,500 pounds of explosives parked below the window of UN representative De Mello's office.

When the truck exploded, 22 people died, including De Mello. A day later I visit friends who work for Unicef. There is a cloud of utter despair. The usually so energetic and pugnacious Iraqi, Ban Dhahi, stares straight ahead and doesn't utter a word. Her Canadian colleague Chris Klein-Beckman has died. Spokesman Geoffrey Keele has been facing the media for days. He steadfastly relates the story of the attack and UN policy, time and again. But when the cameras stop rolling, Geoffrey looks around confused. 'I've got to get out of here. To a tropical island, I want to drink myself into oblivion for two weeks. And once the hangover clears, then I need two weeks to think about my life. I have never seen Geoffrey, who lived with his wife in Baghdad, in Iraq again, and the Unicef office is still closed. 'I hate Dildo Palace!' A 20-year-old American soldier has just returned from patrol and throws his helmet into the room. 'Shit! No electricity or air conditioning again.' When he takes off his bulletproof vest, his uniform is soaked in sweat. He stinks, and his mates hardly smell fresh either. No wonder, because it is the height of summer in Iraq. The temperature is about 58°C, your socks swim out of your shoes.

It seemed like it would be wonderful for the men from the 4th Infantry Brigade when they moved into this majestic palace of Saddam's murdered son. Úday, the sadistic playboy, most certainly had a good time there. The palace stands on a high rock above the river Tigris and, so the gardener says, was used as a brothel. Former servants say that Uday sometimes chartered a whole airplane of whores from Russia. Beneath mile-high chandeliers in marble rooms there are enormous four-poster beds where the American soldiers found dozens of dildos when they moved in. But Dildo Palace, as the soldiers call Uday's den of iniquity, has lost a lot of its lustre.

The bathrooms have gold taps and marble baths, but they stink of rank urine and encrusted shit because there is no running water. In the indoor swimming pool there is a thick layer of brown desert dust but not a drop of water. The soldiers who hunt down 'terrorists' in the heat of the day return exhausted. They don't have the energy to go and bathe in the river. Some of them don't even take the trouble to remove their dirty socks and immediately fall asleep on their camp- beds in the smouldering heat.

We Got Him

Bremer's bodyguards look tough and dangerous. They are athletically built men, armed to the teeth with machine guns, pistols and knives. They always wear a bulletproof vest and have an earpiece in their left ear. The highest authority in Iraq is the top target for terrorists and the Iraqi insurgency. This means he has even better protection than the American President. Even when Bremer goes to the toilet, ten metres from his office, he is accompanied by four men.

Paul Bremer is in a hurry, since America's new MacArthur only has five months to put Iraq on track, and then the Iraqis have to take the strain themselves. It's all go in Bremer's cramped office. 'Schedule, schedule, schedule - that is what I want,' calls out Bremer. He has already overseen 1,750 projects. Bremer is evidently a man with an unbelievable amount of factual knowledge. Whether it concerns the oil industry or the process of emancipation for women in Iraq, Bremer knows all the data and percentages by heart and corrects his employees in a friendly manner. Bremer, as brilliant as he is stubborn, decides at a moment's notice, without any hesitation. For amounts greater than US$ 100 million he needs Washington's rubber stamp. About three times a week he talks with Condoleezza Rice in the White House on the red telephone. He eats from a plastic plate next to his computer and he doesn't even have time to pour the can of soda into a glass.

Bremer lives in 'the castle' within a bell-glass. In an enclave with about 1,000 Americans, he lives a life isolated from Iraq in the 'green zone'. If Bremer ever comes into contact with Iraqis then it is in his office or at prearranged and heavily guarded meetings. It is noteworthy that the Iraqis are terribly nice to him at these meetings, and they almost all want to pose for a photo with him. Luckily, Bremer still seems to have some sense of reality. If an Iraqi woman cheers him enthusiastically 'Iraqis like you Mr. Bremer!' - then Bremer replies with a snigger: 'Yeah, the ones that are not trying to kill me!'

It is five in the morning and the soldiers want to get back to base before sunrise. The six people they have arrested shuffle, blindfolded and handcuffed, to the Bradley armoured vehicle that will take them to an improvised prison. One of the men arrested at the hotel is bleeding heavily from his nose and mouth. 'He stumbled,' the soldiers say." Apparently there was nobody near him to catch him. Not a jot of sympathy. At first glance it looks like the man has a broken nose and has lost a tooth. Despite his agonizing pain, the man has to sit in the cramped Bradley, in a squat position and tightly handcuffed. It's true that the platoon's nurse, Doc, has placed a bandage on the man's head, but he doesn't lift another finger. While the man is driven off, a soldier says: "There, you Fedayeen asshole. That's wiped the smile off your face.'

In the wintry freezing cold, the six prisoners sit squatting and in handcuffs at the barbed-wire entrance to the prison on the base. Three men are barefoot and wear only their pyjamas. The 'jail' is a dusty, empty store. There are a hundred men sitting on a concrete floor. Only a few of them have a mattress. A biting wind blows through the high sliding doors that are standing wide open.

Captain Brown's soldiers have seen just about everything: Saddam- Fedayeen, loyalists of the Ba'ath party, roadside bombs, mortar and grenade attacks and intensive shoot-outs with the Iraqi insurgency. Their own living conditions are abysmal. Survival is their first priority. As a group they have become so close that excesses usually go unpun- ished. 'What else could happen to us?' some of them ask. 'Send us to Iraq as a penalty?"

In the unbearable heat of August 2003 I was at another prison. It stank of piss and sweat. There were the prisoners, like animals in the dirt behind barbed wire, guarded by soldiers with cudgels. The commander thought it was a disgraceful scene, but he didn't know where else he could hold the detainees. Things could be worse, however. 'You should be there when I hand over these people to the base in Tikrit for questioning. Then the soldiers jab their boots in the prisoners' backs to yank the handcuffs as tight as possible around their wrists. I don't want to know what else is going on there.'

It's the period when the atrocities in the Abu Ghraib prison were being perpetrated. Months later, in December, the US Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld has the audacity to say that the prisoners in Iraq were treated 'very, very well'. The questions about the whys and wherefores of the war begin to sound louder and clearer, and no longer just in Iraq.

Iraqis Proclaim
Victory as US
leaves Fallujah

Close by Fallujah, two of the American military's Apache helicopters fly low above our shabby truck for a few minutes - one just in front of us, the other just behind. You can almost touch them and you can make a tally of the munitions: rockets, grenades, rounds for the chain guns. We slow down and breathe a sigh of relief as the two dangerous 'bees' fly on.

I call my contact 'Hassan' in Fallujah from the car. I stick the antenna of the satellite telephone through a chink in the window. Nobody stops on this road, because it is too dangerous. Today, Hassan is bringing me into contact with the insurgency.

I meet him at the agreed place. He gets in and has to surrender his passport and car keys. He will get them back if he brings me back safe and sound. Hassan and I get into a car where two unfamiliar men are sitting waiting. They belong to the local insurgency.

The two men get out of the car. After a couple of minutes they wave me into a building. Then three men come in, each of them dragging a jute sack. They have grenade launchers, RPGs, standard grenades and guns in them. The men pull balaclavas over their heads and don head- bands with a text from the Koran - including the man who was just so appreciative of the lingerie photo. They pick up the grenades, screw them into the rocket launcher and stare into the lens.

Four minutes later we hear helicopters fly over. Tense, the men listen, but the helicopters don't leave. They lay their weapons on the floor and grab me, frisking me and searching my camera bag. The helicopters fly over again. The militiamen take to their heels. I am pushed back into the car. We drive away cautiously. The man beside me is extremely nervous and is sweating like a pig. Fortunately I am escorted back to my car. Hassan gets back his passport and keys, and we drive back to Baghdad.

Hospitals Need
Treatment

During the Saddam years many intellectuals fled the country. Six thousand doctors went abroad and, according to the surgeon Quralsh al Kasim, not one of them has returned to help. Medies have a hard time in Iraq. New victims arrive every day.

The mortuary is full, every day. The pathologist Hassan Faisal Lazim says that 95 per cent of the victims are killed by a bullet: 20 per cent of the time by the coalition troops, otherwise among themselves. The police are completely ineffective. Everyone has a firearm. Before the war, the murder rate in Baghdad was about 75 per month. In August 2003, 1200 people were killed in attacks, revenge killings and other disputes.

It is 10:30 a.m., and Lazim has already performed 22 post- mortems. The surgeon takes a short break and sips on a cup of tea. 'Since the bomb attacks on the UN and the Red Cross, all the big aid organizations have left the country. Look at my hospital. What a load of old rubbish, what terrible equipment! While here it is a state of emergency every day. Nobody's life is safe in this country. Mine neither. Often I sleep with colleagues in the hospital. It is too danger- ous to walk on the street at night, even though I live only five minutes from the hospital.'

Final Notice

War is a harrowing testament to humanity's darkest tendencies. It is a stark reminder of our capacity for violence, destruction, and suffering on a grand scale. The horrors of war are multifaceted, encompassing both the physical and psychological dimensions of conflict.

One of the most immediate and gruesome aspects of war is the loss of life. Soldiers and civilians alike are thrust into the throes of violence, often with no control over their fate. The toll of death and injury is staggering, leaving families shattered and communities scarred. The brutality of war knows no bounds, as people are subjected to unimaginable pain and suffering.

Beyond the physical toll, war leaves deep emotional wounds that may never fully heal. Post-traumatic stress, anxiety, and depression become lifelong companions for many who survive. The mental anguish endured by combatants and civilians, especially children, can be just as crippling as physical injuries.

War also disrupts entire societies, causing displacement, famine, and economic devastation. It tears apart the social fabric, sowing seeds of hatred and vengeance that can persist for generations. The ravages of war poison the land, leaving it riddled with mines and toxins, affecting ecosystems and endangering lives long after the conflict has ended.

In the modern age, technology has amplified the horrors of war. The destructive power of weaponry is unparalleled, capable of obliterating entire cities and causing irreversible environmental damage. Nuclear weapons, in particular, loom as the ultimate testament to humanity's potential for self-annihilation.

War is a costly venture, both in terms of human lives and economic resources. Nations often divert vast sums of money from education, healthcare, and social programs to fund their military endeavors, perpetuating cycles of poverty and inequality.

In the face of these grim realities, it becomes essential to work towards peace, to prevent conflicts before they escalate into wars, and to find non-violent means of resolving disputes. Only by acknowledging the horrors of war can we strive for a world where diplomacy and cooperation are prioritized, and the devastating aspects of armed conflict are confined to history's darkest chapters.
